KHMER

The traditional materials used in Khmer crafts and architecture include sandstone for detailed carvings on monuments like Angkor Wat, laterite for foundations, wood for residential and palace structures, and natural fibers like silk, cotton, palm, and bamboo for textiles and household objects. Precious metals such as silver and gold are used in royal and ceremonial items.


The History

Khmer history is defined by the powerful Khmer Empire (c. 802-1431), which built impressive cities like Angkor and dominated mainland Southeast Asia, leaving a legacy of magnificent temples such as Angkor Wat. Following the empire's decline, Cambodia experienced centuries of conflict, French colonization, and later, the devastating Khmer Rouge regime (1975-1979), which resulted in the deaths of millions. Today, Cambodia is a constitutional monarchy that joined ASEAN in 1999. The history of Cambodia, a country in mainland Southeast Asia, begins with the earliest evidence of habitation around 5000 BCE. Detailed records of a political structure on the territory of what is now Cambodia first appear in Chinese annals in reference to Funan, a polity that encompassed the southernmost part of the Indochinese peninsula during the 1st to 6th centuries.


Buddha

Khmer Buddha refers to the style of Buddhist art and the predominant practice of Theravada Buddhism in Cambodia. This style is known for its unique artistic elements, such as adorned statues with monastic robes draped in the "khlum" style, which clings closely to the body. Khmer statues are also recognized for their serene, smiling expressions and unique ornamentation, like stylized rosettes and pendants.
